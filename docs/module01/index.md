# Module 01 — Introduction to Fluid Power Systems

## The machine you are building

Picture a machine that has to **lift a two-tonne load** — and the space you have for the lifting mechanism is about the size of a shoebox. A steel cylinder no wider than a coffee cup pushes the load up; a small motor-and-pump unit sits to the side, connected only by a hose. That hose carries pressurized fluid, and the fluid is what does the heavy lifting.

That is the machine this course builds. You will start with this simple lifting machine and, module by module, grow it into a complete, controlled hydraulic system. Before anything else, you should be able to **sketch it**: a power unit, a hose, a cylinder, and a load.

## What it does, and why it exists

The machine converts a small amount of rotary power (a motor turning a pump) into a very large, controllable, straight-line force (a cylinder moving a load). It exists because some jobs — pressing, lifting, clamping, digging — need **more force than a compact electric or mechanical drive can deliver**, and need to **hold** that force steadily without strain. Fluid power is how excavators, presses, aircraft controls, and farm equipment move the world.

## What you will design in this module

Across four short lessons you will build one thing: a **System Concept Diagram** — your own clear sketch of the machine, showing where energy enters, how it travels, and how it becomes force at the load. Each lesson adds one piece of understanding to that diagram.

| Lesson | The question it answers |
|--------|--------------------------|
| 01 — Why fluid power exists | Why use pressurized fluid at all, instead of a motor and gears? |
| 02 — How energy is transmitted | How does power get from the unit to the load? |
| 03 — Force multiplication | How does a small push become a huge force? |
| 04 — Real industrial machines | Where is this used, and what does it look like in the field? |

## What you do in the Trainer

The Trainer is your laboratory. In this module you use it to **see the machine** — to watch how a power unit, a fluid line, and a cylinder fit together, and to feel how force appears at the load. You are not memorizing parts; you are learning to make the first design decision: *is fluid power the right choice for this machine?*

## Outcome

By the end of Module 01 you can explain what fluid power is, recognize where it is used, and sketch the machine you are about to build — clearly enough that someone else could understand it from your diagram alone.
